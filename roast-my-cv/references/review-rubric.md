# Resume Review Rubric

Use this rubric to evaluate how well a resume fits a target job description and how effectively it sells the candidate.

## Core Principle

A strong resume is not a biography. It is a positioning document for a specific role.

Judge the resume on how clearly it answers:
- Why this candidate for this role?
- Why now?
- What evidence supports that claim?

## Scoring Dimensions

Score each dimension mentally, then translate into the final `Overall Fit Score`.

### 1. Role Alignment

Check whether the resume reads like the type of candidate the employer is trying to hire.

Look for:
- Market-facing job titles
- Relevant seniority
- Relevant domain exposure
- Relevant scope of work

Penalize:
- Titles that are too internal, vague, or academic
- Experience that is real but framed for the wrong audience

### 2. Keyword And ATS Match

Check whether the document clearly overlaps with the job description in wording, technologies, and category language.

Look for:
- Tools and technologies named in the JD
- Function words used by the employer
- Industry language that makes the overlap obvious

Penalize:
- Missing keywords that should naturally appear
- Generic language instead of role-specific terminology

### 3. Evidence And Impact

Prefer measurable outcomes over responsibility statements.

Strong bullets usually contain:
- A concrete action
- A business, product, or technical result
- Scope, scale, or a metric

Weak bullets usually sound like:
- "Responsible for..."
- "Helped with..."
- "Worked on..."
- "Provided support..."

### 4. Clarity And Compression

Every bullet should earn its space.

Look for:
- Tight wording
- Fast readability
- Low fluff
- Strong verbs

Penalize:
- Repetition
- Empty adjectives
- Long, soft, non-committal phrasing

### 5. Story And Positioning

The resume should tell a coherent market story.

Look for:
- Clear direction
- Logical career narrative
- Summary that sounds specific rather than templated
- Short and concise, 1-2 lines max.

Penalize:
- Generic summaries
- Mixed signals about role focus
- Random low-value sections that weaken the document

## Rewrite Rules

When rewriting, follow these rules:
- Mirror important JD language without blindly copying it
- Convert responsibilities into achievements when evidence exists
- Add numbers when the candidate likely has them and should include them
- Normalize unclear titles into conventional ones
- Remove sections that take space without helping the target role
- Keep bullets short, sharp, and outcome-oriented

## Good Signals

Good resumes usually:
- Match the target role quickly
- Use standard titles
- Quantify outcomes
- Show ownership
- Show progression
- Read cleanly in under a minute

## Red Flags

Watch for:
- Generic executive-summary boilerplate
- No metrics anywhere
- Bullets that describe work without outcomes
- Jargon-heavy but evidence-light content
- Unclear seniority
- Unexplained career pivots
- Keyword mismatch against the JD
- Strange section choices that waste space

## Question Strategy

Only ask follow-up questions after giving the score and core analysis.

Ask questions that unlock stronger rewrites, such as:
- What was the measurable outcome?
- How many users, customers, teammates, or systems were affected?
- What tools did you use?
- Was this ownership, collaboration, or support?
- What is the market-facing equivalent of this title?
